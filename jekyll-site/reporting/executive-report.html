Executive Report

Who Reads the Executive Summary

The executive summary is written for CISOs, VPs of Engineering, CEOs, and board members — people who make budget decisions and set risk tolerance. They don't read technical findings. They read the first two pages. Your job in the executive summary is to answer three questions clearly: How bad is it? What can attackers do to us? What are we doing about it?

Assume zero technical knowledge. Never use acronyms without spelling them out. Lead with business impact, not attack technique names.

// executive report structure — business-first narrative for non-technical leadership

Executive Summary Structure

An executive summary runs 1–3 pages and covers five sections in order.

># EXECUTIVE SUMMARY STRUCTURE

1. ENGAGEMENT OVERVIEW (1 paragraph)
   ─────────────────────────────────
   Who engaged you, when, what was in scope, what type of assessment
   (black-box, gray-box, assumed breach). Set context for non-technical
   readers. Include the assessment dates and lead consultant name.

   Example:
   "From March 15–27, 2026, Acme Corp engaged [Firm] to conduct a
   full-scope red team assessment simulating an external threat actor
   with no prior knowledge of the environment. The assessment targeted
   Acme's corporate network, cloud infrastructure, and active directory
   environment across two geographic locations."

2. OVERALL SECURITY POSTURE (1–2 paragraphs)
   ────────────────────────────────────────────
   Your honest assessment of the organization's security maturity.
   High-level: strong, moderate, weak. What worked. What didn't.
   Avoid grades (A/B/C) — they invite argument. Use narrative.

3. KEY FINDINGS SUMMARY (bullet list)
   ────────────────────────────────────
   Top 5–8 findings described in plain language.
   Every bullet = business impact first, attack detail second.

   Bad:  "Identified Kerberoasting vulnerability in AD"
   Good: "An attacker with a standard employee account could obtain
         administrator-level access to all domain systems within 30
         minutes using a freely available tool."

4. ATTACK NARRATIVE (1–2 paragraphs)
   ────────────────────────────────────
   Tell the story of the attack path. No technical jargon.
   Focus on what an attacker could have done — data accessed,
   systems controlled, business functions disrupted.

5. RECOMMENDATIONS (prioritized list)
   ────────────────────────────────────
   3–5 top-priority items. Match technical findings to business
   priorities. Frame as: "If you do only three things, do these."

Business Impact Framing

Every technical finding maps to a business risk. Make that mapping explicit in the executive summary — don't leave it for the reader to infer.

># Technical → Business impact translation examples:

Domain Admin compromise →
  "An attacker would have unrestricted access to all systems, data,
   and communications across the organization. Recovery from a
   ransomware attack originating from this position would require
   a complete rebuild of the Active Directory environment — estimated
   at 3–6 weeks of downtime for an organization of this size."

SQL injection on customer portal →
  "Customer account data, including names, email addresses, and
   hashed passwords for approximately 45,000 accounts, was accessible
   to an unauthenticated attacker. Depending on jurisdiction, this
   may trigger breach notification requirements under GDPR or CCPA."

Weak VPN credentials →
  "An attacker could gain direct access to the internal corporate
   network from the internet, bypassing the perimeter firewall
   entirely. This is the same entry point that enabled the 2021
   Colonial Pipeline ransomware attack."

Unpatched public-facing web server →
  "The outdated software version used on the company's public website
   has a known vulnerability that can be exploited by an automated
   scanner with no human involvement."

Risk Rating and Posture Summary

Give the executive a single-sentence or single-number answer to "how bad is it?" Use a clear rating system and define it in the report.

># Overall posture ratings (choose one framework and use it consistently):

Option A — Narrative rating:
  Critical Risk | High Risk | Moderate Risk | Low Risk | Acceptable Risk

Option B — Maturity level:
  Level 1 (Reactive) | Level 2 (Developing) | Level 3 (Defined) |
  Level 4 (Managed)  | Level 5 (Optimized)

Option C — Simple stoplight:
  RED   — Significant vulnerabilities with active exploitation potential
  AMBER — Vulnerabilities present; exploitation requires additional steps
  GREEN — Vulnerabilities present but limited practical impact

Top-Line Remediation Priorities

Executives need to know what to tell their teams to fix first. Provide a short, prioritized list — no more than five items — with rough effort estimates.

># PRIORITIZED REMEDIATION FOR EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Priority 1 — IMMEDIATE (this week):
  Apply patch for CVE-2026-XXXX on the VPN concentrator.
  This closes the external entry point used in our assessment.
  Estimated effort: 2–4 hours. Requires maintenance window.

Priority 2 — IMMEDIATE (this week):
  Reset all service account passwords to 25+ character random values.
  Implement group Managed Service Accounts (gMSA) on a rolling basis.
  Estimated effort: 4–8 hours per account migration.

Priority 3 — SHORT-TERM (30 days):
  Enforce multi-factor authentication on all VPN and remote access.
  Estimated effort: 1–2 weeks depending on MFA vendor selection.

Priority 4 — SHORT-TERM (60 days):
  Conduct privileged access review — identify all accounts with
  Domain Admin and remove unnecessary memberships.
  Estimated effort: 1–2 weeks with Active Directory team.

Priority 5 — MEDIUM-TERM (90 days):
  Deploy endpoint detection and response (EDR) across all workstations.
  Current coverage: 40%. Target: 100%.
  Estimated effort: 6–8 weeks for full deployment.

Example Executive Summary — Full Draft

># ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
# EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
# Red Team Assessment — Acme Corporation
# Assessment Period: March 15–27, 2026
# ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

ENGAGEMENT OVERVIEW

From March 15–27, 2026, Acme Corporation engaged [Firm] to conduct
a full-scope adversarial simulation. Our team operated with no
prior access or insider knowledge, simulating a sophisticated
external threat actor. Testing covered Acme's corporate network,
active directory environment, public-facing web applications, and
Microsoft 365 cloud services.

OVERALL SECURITY POSTURE: HIGH RISK

Acme's security controls are insufficient to detect or stop a
motivated attacker. Our team gained domain administrator access —
the highest possible privilege level on a Windows network — within
14 hours of starting the assessment. From that position, we had
unrestricted access to all employee systems, email, file shares,
and databases. The controls that were in place (perimeter firewall,
basic antivirus) provided minimal resistance to the techniques
commonly used by ransomware groups and nation-state actors.

The areas of strongest security posture were patch management for
externally-facing systems and network segmentation for the payment
processing environment. The areas requiring urgent attention are
internal network defenses, service account security, and detection
capabilities.

KEY FINDINGS

• An attacker with a standard employee account can become a domain
  administrator in under 15 minutes using freely available tools.
  No alerts were triggered during the escalation process.

• Credentials found in an IT administrator's email allowed access
  to the company's backup system and cloud storage, containing
  copies of the payroll database and HR records.

• Fourteen servers accessible from the internet are running software
  with known, publicly documented vulnerabilities that can be
  exploited without authentication.

• Multi-factor authentication is not enforced for remote access,
  allowing any compromised employee password to provide direct
  corporate network access.

RECOMMENDATIONS (Top 3)

1. Immediate: Enforce multi-factor authentication on all remote access
   and privileged accounts. This single control would have stopped
   or significantly delayed our initial access.

2. Immediate: Rotate all service account passwords and implement
   automated rotation. These accounts were the primary escalation
   path to domain administrator.

3. Within 60 days: Deploy endpoint detection and response (EDR)
   across all endpoints. We operated undetected for the entire
   assessment — modern EDR would have generated alerts at multiple
   stages of the attack chain.
